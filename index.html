Impact Wrench Unboxing Instructions

When your impact wrench arrives, follow this step-by-step guide to ensure everything is correct and ready for use.

1. Check the condition of the packaging

Before opening, make sure the box has no tears, deep dents, or signs of tampering. This helps confirm the product arrived safely.

2. Open the box carefully

Use scissors or a utility knife with caution so you don’t damage any items inside. Open the box from the sides while keeping it in a horizontal position.

3. Gently remove all contents

Inside the box, you will find:

  • The impact wrench
  • Battery or power source, depending on the model
  • Charger
  • Carrying case or storage box (if included)
  • Instruction manual
  • Additional accessories such as bits, sockets, or adapters

Place everything on a flat surface to make inspection easier.

4. Confirm that all items are included

Compare what came in the box with the official list in the manual. If anything is missing, contact support immediately.

5. Perform a quick visual inspection

Check the tool for scratches, loose parts, or any visible damage. Also ensure the battery is intact.

6. Read the manual before first use

Even if you already have experience with similar tools, read the manual carefully to understand the functions, operating modes, and safety guidelines.

7. Prepare the tool for its first use

If your model uses a battery, charge it fully before using the tool for the first time. This helps extend the battery’s long-term lifespan.

8. Keep the original packaging

It is recommended to keep the box and internal materials, at least during the warranty period. This helps with returns or exchanges if needed.

First Battery Charge Instructions

Performing the first charge correctly is one of the most important steps to ensure that your lithium battery delivers maximum performance.

1. Remove the battery from the tool

Press the release buttons firmly and slide the battery out. Charging the battery outside the tool prevents accidental activation and ensures proper temperature control during the process.

2. Use only the original charger included in your kit

Each charger is designed to deliver the correct voltage and current for its specific battery model. Using incompatible chargers can overload the cells, cause overheating, or drastically reduce battery life.

3. Charge the battery fully before using the tool for the first time

Connect the battery to the charger until the indicator light displays a full charge. A complete first charge helps the internal management system calibrate and ensures the battery reaches its intended storage and working capacity.

4. Do not interrupt the first charge cycle

Avoid removing the battery before the initial charge is complete. Interrupting the first cycle may lead to inaccurate calibration and reduce the battery’s ability to hold charge over time.

5. Allow the battery to rest before use

After removing it from the charger, let the battery sit for approximately 5 minutes. This resting period allows the internal cells to stabilize their temperature and voltage before being placed under load.

Essential Guidelines to Maximize Lithium Battery Lifespan

Lithium batteries are highly efficient and durable when used correctly. Following the recommendations below will significantly extend their operating life and protect their charging capacity.

1. Avoid deep discharges whenever possible

Lithium batteries do not need to be drained completely. Allowing the charge to drop below 20 percent repeatedly increases internal stress and accelerates aging of the cells.

2. Do not leave the battery charging for long periods after it reaches 100 percent

Although lithium batteries have internal protection circuits, prolonged overcharging generates unnecessary heat, which gradually degrades the cells.

3. Store the battery under the right conditions

If you plan to store the battery for several weeks or months:

  • Keep it between 40 and 60 percent charge.
  • Store it in a cool, dry environment.
  • Avoid any temperature above 35°C or below 0°C.

Extreme temperatures are one of the main causes of premature battery deterioration.

4. Use the battery regularly

Lithium batteries benefit from periodic use. If the tool stays unused, the battery may enter deep sleep mode, reducing its responsiveness. Running the tool briefly every few weeks helps keep the cells active.

5. Keep battery contacts clean and free of debris

Use a dry cloth to clean the metal contacts occasionally. Dust, grease, or oxidation can interfere with energy transfer and slow down charging.

6. Allow the battery to cool before charging

Charging a hot battery causes chemical stress and increases internal resistance, reducing the number of usable cycles. If the battery warms up during use, let it cool to room temperature before attaching it to the charger.

7. Protect the battery from moisture and impacts

Water, oil, or cleaning products can damage the internal circuit board and permanently affect battery safety. Also avoid drops or strong impacts, as lithium cells are sensitive to deformation.

Torque Adjustment Instructions

Correct torque adjustment is essential to ensure safety, avoid damage to components, and improve the tool’s performance.

1. Identify the torque control system of your impact wrench

Most models offer:

  • Multiple speed levels
  • Torque presets
  • Variable trigger pressure for fine control

Understanding these settings will help you adjust the tool accurately.

2. Choose the correct torque level for your task

Low torque: ideal for small screws, delicate materials, and assembly work where precision is important.

Medium torque: recommended for automotive tasks, household repairs, and general fastening.

High torque: used for heavy-duty applications such as loosening tight bolts, construction work, and rusted or seized fasteners.

3. Start with a lower torque setting

When working on unfamiliar materials or hardware, begin at the lowest power level. Gradually increase until the screw or bolt is tightened securely without over-tightening.

4. Avoid excessive torque

Applying too much torque can damage threads, crack components, or make future removal difficult. Use proper socket sizes and always keep the tool aligned with the fastener to prevent slipping.

5. Test torque on a sample piece when possible

If you are working on sensitive materials or new parts, test the torque setting on a spare piece before applying it to the final job.

How to Set the Correct Power Supply for Different Types of Work

1. Understand your tool’s power system

Depending on the model, your impact wrench may operate on lithium-ion battery packs or external power adapters. Most modern impact wrenches rely on rechargeable batteries, so proper battery management is essential.

2. For small or medium tasks, use a partially charged battery

For light work such as furniture assembly or general home use, a battery between 50% and 80% is sufficient. This helps preserve long-term battery health while still delivering consistent performance.

3. For heavy-duty jobs, ensure a fully charged battery

Tasks like removing wheel nuts, working on machinery, or loosening rusted bolts require maximum power output. Always start with a fully charged battery for these situations to avoid power drops during operation.

4. Avoid using the tool when the battery is below 20%

Low voltage reduces torque, causes the tool to strain, and accelerates battery wear. Recharge once the battery reaches approximately 20–30%.

5. Use the correct charger and allow full charge cycles

The original charger provides the exact voltage and current your battery requires. Using third-party chargers may lead to overheating, inconsistent power delivery, or long-term damage.

6. Keep a spare battery for uninterrupted work

If you frequently work on heavy jobs or long projects, having a second battery ensures continuous power. Alternate between batteries to maintain healthy charge cycles.

7. Store batteries properly when not in use

Keep the battery between 40% and 60% charge, away from heat, moisture, or direct sunlight. Correct storage guarantees safer and more efficient operation during future tasks.

How to Properly Use Your Impact Wrench

Using your impact wrench correctly ensures maximum performance, safety, and durability. Follow this step-by-step guide to operate the tool efficiently, and refer to the recommended videos provided below for visual demonstrations.

1. Understand the Basic Controls

Before using the tool, familiarize yourself with:

  • The trigger
  • Forward and reverse switch
  • Speed and torque controls
  • Battery release button
  • Socket connection system

Knowing these controls makes operation smoother and prevents misuse.

2. Choose the Correct Socket Size

Always select a socket that fits tightly onto the bolt or nut. Loose-fitting sockets can cause slipping, rounding of bolts, or accidents. Make sure the socket is properly locked onto the tool before starting.

3. Set the Direction of Rotation

Use the forward/reverse switch to choose:

  • Forward (tightening)
  • Reverse (loosening)

Always check the direction before pulling the trigger.

4. Adjust Speed and Torque Levels

Your impact wrench may offer multiple modes or speed settings. Use them as follows:

  • Low speed / low torque: Furniture, small screws, delicate materials
  • Medium speed: Automotive tasks, general household repairs
  • High speed / high torque: Wheel nuts, construction bolts, rusted or tight fasteners

Start with a lower setting and increase only if necessary. Overtightening can damage threads or components.

5. Proper Grip and Positioning

Hold the tool firmly with both hands when possible. Keep your wrist aligned with the bolt to prevent slipping. Position the socket straight onto the fastener to ensure efficient torque transfer.

6. Use Short Controlled Bursts

Instead of holding the trigger continuously, apply short bursts: This gives you better control and prevents overtightening.

7. Remove the Fastener Carefully

When loosening bolts, keep the tool aligned. As soon as the fastener is free, release the trigger to avoid spinning it out unexpectedly.

8. Check Bolt Tightness with a Torque Wrench (Optional)

For critical applications such as:

  • Car wheels
  • Machinery
  • Structural components

Always finish tightening with a manual torque wrench. Impact wrenches provide power, but torque wrenches provide precision.

9. Battery Management While Using the Tool

  • Start heavy-duty tasks with a fully charged battery
  • Avoid using the tool below 20% charge
  • Switch batteries when you notice reduced performance
  • Allow the battery to cool before recharging

Correct Positioning for Using Your Impact Wrench

1. Maintain a stable stance

Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart to keep balance and prevent unwanted movement while operating the tool.

2. Hold the tool firmly

Use a steady, comfortable grip on the handle. Keep your wrist straight to avoid strain and to improve control of the impact wrench.

3. Align the tool with the fastener

Position the socket perfectly straight over the bolt or nut. Proper alignment ensures full torque transfer and reduces the risk of slipping or damaging the fastener.

4. Support with the other hand when needed

For high-torque tasks or tight spaces, use your free hand to stabilize the front of the tool for better precision and control.

5. Let the tool do the work

Avoid applying sideways pressure or forcing the tool forward. Simply hold it in place and allow the impact mechanism to deliver the necessary power.

Work Cycles, Pause Time, and Usage Limits

1. Understand the tool’s work cycle

Impact wrenches are designed to operate in short, controlled bursts rather than continuous use. A typical work cycle involves applying torque for a few seconds, followed by a brief rest period. This prevents overheating and ensures consistent performance.

2. Avoid prolonged continuous operation

Using the tool nonstop for long periods can cause excess heat in the motor, gearbox, and battery. Always limit each operation to short intervals, especially when working with high torque loads or stubborn fasteners.

3. Allow proper cooling time

If the tool becomes warm during use, pause for several minutes to let it cool down naturally. Forced cooling (like placing it near fans or in cold environments) is not recommended, as rapid temperature changes may affect internal components.

4. Monitor battery temperature

Lithium batteries can heat up during demanding tasks. If the battery becomes hot, remove it and allow it to cool before charging or reinstalling. Operating or charging a hot battery reduces its lifespan and may damage internal cells.

5. Respect usage limits for heavy-duty tasks

When working on jobs that require maximum power, such as loosening tight or rusted bolts, use the tool in short bursts. Overloading the tool by holding the trigger continuously can strain both the motor and impact mechanism.

6. Alternate tasks to prevent overheating

If performing multiple high-torque operations, switch between heavy and light tasks to allow the tool and battery to recover gradually.

7. Stop immediately if you notice performance drop

A sudden decrease in torque, unusual noise, or increased vibration may indicate overheating. Pause use and allow the tool to cool before continuing.

Tool Lubrication: Importance, Frequency, and Proper Application

Proper lubrication is essential to keep your impact wrench running smoothly, extend its lifespan, and ensure consistent performance during demanding tasks. Internal components such as the hammer mechanism, bearings, and moving parts rely on adequate lubrication to reduce friction and prevent premature wear. Understanding how and when to lubricate your tool is an important part of maintaining its long-term reliability.

1. Why lubrication matters

The internal hammering system of an impact wrench generates high levels of force and rapid movement. Without proper lubrication, this friction increases significantly, which can lead to overheating, loss of power, and accelerated component wear. Regular lubrication keeps the parts moving freely and improves efficiency during operation.

2. Identify lubrication points

Most impact wrenches include designated areas where lubrication should be applied, such as the anvil, hammer mechanism, and sometimes the front housing. Always check your model’s manual to confirm the correct lubrication points and recommended grease type.

3. Use the correct lubricant

Impact wrenches require high-quality, high-temperature grease specifically designed for impact mechanisms. Using the wrong type of lubricant can break down under heavy loads or cause internal clogging. Avoid oils or lightweight greases unless explicitly recommended by the manufacturer.

4. How often to lubricate

Frequency depends on usage:

  • Frequent or heavy-duty use: lubricate every few weeks or after large projects
  • Moderate use: lubricate every 1–2 months
  • Occasional use: lubricate every 3–6 months

If the tool begins making unusual noises, feels rough during operation, or loses efficiency, lubrication may be needed sooner.

5. Steps for proper lubrication

  • Disconnect the tool from the battery before servicing
  • Remove the front housing or access cover (if applicable)
  • Clean dust or old grease from visible areas using a dry cloth
  • Apply a small amount of recommended grease directly onto the moving parts
  • Reassemble the tool securely before use

Avoid over-lubricating, as excess grease can trap dust and reduce performance.

6. Keep the anvil lightly lubricated

The anvil—the square drive where sockets attach—experiences constant friction. Apply a small layer of grease occasionally to ensure smooth socket engagement and prevent rust.

7. Watch for signs of poor lubrication

  • Unusual grinding noises
  • Reduced torque or slower performance
  • Excessive heat during short work cycles
  • Increased vibration

If any of these signs appear, stop using the tool and inspect lubrication before continuing.

8. Store the tool properly after lubrication

After servicing, store the impact wrench in a dry environment to prevent grease contamination. Keeping dust and moisture away preserves both the internal mechanism and the lubrication itself.

External Cleaning of Your Impact Wrench

Keeping the exterior of your impact wrench clean helps preserve its durability, prevents dust buildup, and ensures safer operation. Follow these steps to maintain the tool properly without damaging its components.

1. Disconnect the battery before cleaning

Always remove the battery to avoid accidental activation and protect the electronic components from moisture.

2. Use a dry or slightly damp cloth

Wipe the exterior of the tool with a dry cloth to remove dust and debris.

If needed, use a lightly dampened cloth to clean stubborn dirt, but avoid excessive moisture.

3. Avoid harsh chemicals

Do not use solvents, alcohol, gasoline, or abrasive cleaners. These substances can damage the plastic housing, rubber grips, and protective coatings.

4. Use a soft brush for hard-to-reach areas

A small brush or soft paintbrush can help remove dust from vents, crevices, and around the anvil area.

Keep ventilation openings free of debris to prevent overheating.

5. Clean the anvil area carefully

Wipe around the anvil and socket connection point to remove dust, grease buildup, or metal shavings.

Do not insert liquids or sprays inside the tool.

6. Dry the tool completely before reassembly

Make sure no moisture remains on the surface before reinstalling the battery.

7. Store the tool in a clean, dry environment

After cleaning, place the impact wrench in its case or storage area to protect it from dust, humidity, and accidental impacts.

How to Properly Store Your Impact Wrench

Correct storage is essential to preserve the performance, safety, and durability of your impact wrench. Following these guidelines helps protect the tool and its battery from damage caused by moisture, dust, and improper handling.

1. Remove the battery before storage

Always detach the battery from the tool when storing it. This prevents accidental activation and protects the battery contacts.

2. Clean the tool before storing

Make sure the exterior is free of dust, grease, and debris. A clean tool is less likely to develop corrosion or long-term wear.

3. Store in a dry and ventilated place

Choose a location that is clean, dry, and away from moisture or direct sunlight. Excess humidity can damage internal components and electrical contacts.

4. Avoid extreme temperatures

Do not store the impact wrench in very hot or very cold environments. Extreme temperatures can affect both the tool’s internal components and battery lifespan.

5. Store the battery correctly

If the battery will not be used for an extended period, store it separately with a charge level between 40% and 60%. This helps maintain battery health and prevents capacity loss.

6. Use the original case or protective storage

Whenever possible, store the impact wrench in its original case or a protective box. This prevents dust accumulation and protects the tool from impacts.

7. Keep the tool away from heavy objects

Do not place heavy items on top of the tool or its case. Excess pressure may damage the housing or internal components.

8. Check the tool periodically

If the tool is stored for long periods, inspect it occasionally and briefly power it on to ensure proper operation.

Complete User Manual – Drill/Driver

The drill/driver is a multifunctional power tool designed to drill holes, drive screws, and remove fasteners in a wide variety of materials such as wood, metal, plastic, drywall and, in some models, concrete and masonry. Proper use ensures better performance, higher precision, operator safety, and longer tool lifespan.

Important: Read all instructions carefully before using the tool.

1. Understanding the main components

Before operating the drill/driver, familiarize yourself with its main parts:

  • Chuck (where the drill bit or driver bit is installed)
  • Torque adjustment ring
  • Mode selector (drilling, driving, or hammer mode)
  • Variable-speed trigger
  • Forward/reverse switch
  • Ergonomic grip
  • Battery pack or power cord
  • Motor ventilation slots

Knowing these components helps you make correct adjustments and avoid mistakes during operation.

2. Proper preparation before use

Before turning on the tool:

  • Check that the battery is fully charged or that the power cord is in good condition.
  • Make sure the trigger is not pressed.
  • Select the correct drill bit or driver bit for the task and material.
  • Insert the bit into the chuck and tighten it securely, ensuring it is centered.
  • Adjust the torque and operating mode before starting.

Never make adjustments while the tool is powered on.

3. Choosing the correct drill bit or driver bit

Using the proper accessory is essential for safety and efficiency:

  • Wood drill bits: center point with side cutters
  • Metal drill bits: conical tip
  • Masonry drill bits: carbide tip (use only with hammer mode)
  • Driver bits: compatible with screw head types (Phillips, flat, Torx, Allen)

Incorrect or damaged bits may break, damage the material, or cause accidents.

4. Torque and speed adjustment

Torque control regulates the amount of force applied to the screw:

  • Low torque: delicate materials or small screws
  • Medium torque: wood and drywall
  • High torque: harder materials

The trigger allows progressive speed control. Start slowly and increase speed as needed. This improves accuracy and prevents slipping.

5. Using the tool in driver mode

  • Align the driver bit with the screw head.
  • Keep the tool straight against the surface.
  • Start at low speed.
  • Apply steady, moderate pressure.
  • Release the trigger immediately once the screw is fully seated.
  • To remove screws, switch the rotation direction to reverse.

6. Using the tool in drilling mode

  • Mark the drilling point in advance.
  • Position the drill bit perpendicular to the surface.
  • Hold the tool firmly.
  • Begin drilling at low speed to prevent slipping.
  • Gradually increase speed.
  • Do not force the tool; allow the drill bit to do the work.
  • For concrete or masonry, use proper bits and activate hammer mode if available.

7. Proper posture and tool control

  • Use both hands whenever possible.
  • Keep your feet firmly on the ground.
  • Avoid unstable positions.
  • Do not work overhead without proper support.
  • Keep the power cord or battery away from the drilling area.

Proper posture reduces fatigue and increases precision.

8. Safety precautions during use

  • Always wear safety glasses.
  • Use protective gloves when appropriate.
  • Avoid loose clothing, jewelry, or accessories.
  • Do not use the tool in wet or damp environments.
  • Keep children and bystanders away from the work area.
  • Do not touch the drill bit immediately after use, as it may be hot.

9. Continuous use and motor care

  • Take breaks during prolonged use.
  • Keep ventilation openings clear.
  • Stop using the tool if you notice unusual noises, vibration, or burning smells.
  • If the tool overheats, turn it off and allow it to cool down.

These precautions help protect the motor and extend tool life.

10. Procedures after use

  • Turn off the tool.
  • Remove the battery or unplug it from the power source.
  • Allow the drill bit to cool before removing it.
  • Clean the tool with a dry cloth.
  • Store it in a dry, ventilated place, protected from moisture.

11. Basic maintenance and care

  • Clean the chuck regularly.
  • Do not use worn or damaged accessories.
  • Recharge the battery only with the original charger.
  • Avoid drops or strong impacts.
  • If any malfunction is detected, seek authorized technical service.

Preventive maintenance reduces costs and increases durability.

12. Final recommendations

Using the drill/driver correctly not only delivers better results but also ensures operator safety and protects the equipment. Always respect the tool’s limits and use it only for its intended purpose.

Complete User Manual – Rotary Hammer

The rotary hammer is a high-performance power tool designed for drilling into concrete, masonry, stone, and other high-resistance materials, as well as for chiseling and light demolition tasks. Unlike a standard drill, a rotary hammer combines rotation with a pneumatic hammering mechanism, delivering superior efficiency with less effort from the operator.

Proper use ensures safety, accuracy, higher productivity, and extended tool lifespan.

Important: Read all instructions carefully before operating the tool.

1. Understanding the main components

Before using the rotary hammer, become familiar with its main components:

  • SDS Plus or SDS Max chuck
  • Mode selector (drilling, hammer drilling, chiseling)
  • Variable-speed trigger
  • Main handle
  • Adjustable auxiliary handle
  • Depth stop rod
  • Lock-on button (on some models)
  • Motor ventilation system
  • Power cord or battery

Knowing these components helps ensure correct setup and prevents improper use.

2. Preparation before use

Before powering on the tool:

  • Ensure the tool is unplugged or the battery is removed.
  • Check that the power cord or battery is in good condition.
  • Install and adjust the auxiliary handle for better control.
  • Select the correct drill bit or chisel for the task.
  • Insert the accessory into the SDS chuck until it locks securely.
  • Set the depth stop if required.
  • Select the appropriate operating mode before turning the tool on.

Never make adjustments while the tool is powered.

3. Choosing the correct drill bits and chisels

Using the correct accessory is essential for safety and performance:

  • SDS masonry drill bits: for concrete, brick, and stone
  • Pointed chisels: for localized demolition
  • Flat chisels: for removing plaster, tiles, and surface concrete

Never use standard drill bits in a rotary hammer.

4. Selecting operating modes

Most rotary hammers offer three main operating modes:

  • Drilling without hammering: for wood or metal (with proper adapter)
  • Hammer drilling: for concrete, masonry, and stone
  • Chiseling: for tile removal and light demolition

Always select the correct mode before pulling the trigger.

5. How to use hammer drilling mode

  • Mark the drilling location.
  • Position the drill bit perpendicular to the surface.
  • Hold the tool firmly with both hands.
  • Start drilling at low speed.
  • Apply steady, moderate pressure without forcing the tool.
  • Let the pneumatic hammering mechanism do the work.

Excessive pressure reduces efficiency and may damage the tool.

6. How to use chiseling mode

  • Install the appropriate chisel.
  • Adjust the chisel angle if the tool allows.
  • Position the tool at the desired location.
  • Activate the trigger in a controlled manner.
  • Work in short intervals to avoid fatigue and overheating.

This mode is ideal for removing tiles, plaster, and surface concrete.

7. Proper posture and tool control

  • Always use both hands.
  • Keep your feet firmly planted and well balanced.
  • Avoid extended overhead work.
  • Never apply lateral force to the tool.
  • Maintain a safe distance from other people.

Proper posture improves control and reduces the risk of accidents.

8. Safety precautions during use

  • Always wear safety glasses.
  • Use hearing protection due to high noise levels.
  • Wear anti-vibration gloves when possible.
  • Use a dust mask in enclosed or dusty environments.
  • Never operate the tool in wet conditions.
  • Avoid contact with hidden electrical wiring or plumbing.

Safety must always be the top priority.

9. Continuous use and motor care

  • Take regular breaks during prolonged operation.
  • Keep ventilation openings clear at all times.
  • Stop using the tool immediately if unusual noise or vibration occurs.
  • If the tool overheats, turn it off and allow it to cool down.

These precautions protect the motor and hammer mechanism.

10. Procedures after use

  • Disconnect the tool from the power source or remove the battery.
  • Allow the accessory to cool before removal.
  • Clean the tool using a dry cloth.
  • Remove accumulated dust from the chuck.
  • Store the tool in a dry, protected location.

11. Basic maintenance and care

  • Lubricate the SDS shank as recommended by the manufacturer.
  • Do not use damaged or worn accessories.
  • Periodically inspect the power cord or battery.
  • Never disassemble the tool yourself.
  • Seek authorized service when necessary.

Preventive maintenance increases durability and reduces repair costs.

12. Final recommendations

The rotary hammer is a powerful tool intended for heavy-duty applications. Proper operation ensures maximum efficiency, reduced physical effort, and operator safety. Always use the correct mode, accessories, and follow recommended operating practices.

Complete User Manual – Circular Saw

The circular saw is a power tool designed to perform straight, precise, and fast cuts in materials such as wood, MDF, plywood, OSB, plastics, and, with the proper blade, light metals. Correct use ensures operator safety, clean cuts, efficient material use, and longer tool lifespan.

Read all instructions carefully before using the tool.

1. Understanding the main components

Before operating the circular saw, familiarize yourself with its main components:

  • Saw blade
  • Retractable lower blade guard
  • Base plate (shoe)
  • Cutting depth adjustment
  • Bevel (angle) adjustment
  • Power trigger
  • Trigger safety lock
  • Main handle
  • Auxiliary handle (on some models)
  • Dust exhaust port
  • Power cord or battery

Understanding these components is essential for proper adjustments and safe operation.

2. Preparation before use

Before turning on the circular saw:

  • Ensure the tool is unplugged or the battery is removed.
  • Check that the blade is properly installed, secure, and in good condition.
  • Confirm the blade is properly suited for the material being cut.
  • Adjust the cutting depth so the blade extends only slightly beyond the material thickness.
  • Set the bevel angle if making angled cuts.
  • Make sure the blade guard moves freely and returns automatically.
  • Secure the workpiece firmly using clamps or supports.

Never make adjustments while the tool is powered on.

3. Choosing the correct saw blade

Selecting the proper blade directly affects safety and cut quality:

  • Wood blades: large, widely spaced teeth
  • Fine-finish or MDF blades: higher tooth count
  • Plastic blades: designed to prevent melting
  • Metal blades: specific blades rated for metal cutting (when permitted)

Never use damaged, warped, or incompatible blades.

4. Adjusting cutting depth and bevel angle

  • Cutting depth: set the blade to extend approximately 5–10 mm (1/4 in) below the material.
  • Bevel angle: adjust the base plate for 45° cuts or as required.

Proper adjustment reduces effort, improves finish quality, and enhances safety.

5. How to make straight cuts

  • Mark the cutting line clearly on the material.
  • Place the saw base on the workpiece without touching the blade to the material.
  • Hold the saw firmly with both hands.
  • Pull the trigger and allow the blade to reach full speed.
  • Guide the saw forward smoothly, keeping the base flat against the surface.
  • Do not force the cut. Let the blade cut at its own pace.

Maintaining control and steady speed helps prevent binding and kickback.

6. How to make bevel (angled) cuts

  • Adjust the base plate to the desired angle.
  • Make sure the angle adjustment is securely locked.
  • Follow the same procedure used for straight cuts, maintaining extra attention to alignment and control.

Bevel cuts require increased stability and precision.

7. Proper posture and tool control

  • Always use both hands.
  • Keep your feet firmly planted and well balanced.
  • Stand slightly to the side of the blade, never directly behind it.
  • Do not cross your arms while cutting.
